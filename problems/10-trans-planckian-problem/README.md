# #10 — The trans-Planckian problem of Hawking radiation

*Also known as: trans-Planckian problem, robustness of Hawking radiation, ultrashort-distance problem.*

> **Status: OPEN WITH RECENT PROGRESS** · Statement tier: `established_theory` · Resolution tier: `open_conjecture` · First posed: 1981-1991.

## The problem

In the standard derivation, the Hawking quanta seen at late times are traced back to modes that were, near the horizon at early times, blueshifted to arbitrarily high (trans-Planckian) frequencies — far beyond where the underlying field theory and the fixed-background approximation can be trusted. The trans-Planckian problem asks whether Hawking radiation is a genuine, robust prediction or an artifact of extrapolating physics into an unknown ultraviolet regime. Analogue-gravity systems and modified-dispersion models give strong evidence for robustness, but a fully satisfactory, first-principles demonstration awaits the true short-distance theory.

## History & originators

- **William Unruh** — 1981 raised the issue and proposed the sonic (analogue) horizon as a testbed.
- **Ted Jacobson** — 1991 analysis of Hawking radiation and ultrashort distances / modified dispersion.

## Key ideas

- Near-horizon exponential blueshift sends the origin of late Hawking quanta to trans-Planckian frequencies in the naive calculation.
- Modified-dispersion-relation models (introducing a UV cutoff) still reproduce a thermal spectrum to good approximation — evidence of robustness.
- Analogue gravity: sonic/optical/BEC 'horizons' realize the same kinematics with KNOWN microphysics, providing experimental tests.
- Observation of stimulated and (claimed) spontaneous analogue Hawking radiation in BECs (Steinhauer) supports the effect's robustness.
- The result hints that Hawking radiation is a low-energy, horizon-kinematics phenomenon insensitive to UV details — but this is argued, not proven.
- Connects to whether the equivalence principle plus QFT alone fix the radiation, independent of quantum gravity.

## Live approaches

- **Modified dispersion relations** (`numerical_approximation`) — Impose a Lorentz-violating UV cutoff and check the spectrum survives. _Proponents: Ted Jacobson, Renaud Parentani, Stefano Liberati._
- **Analogue gravity (sonic/optical/BEC horizons)** (`observational_constraint`) — Reproduce horizon kinematics in condensed-matter systems with known microphysics; measure analogue Hawking radiation. _Proponents: William Unruh, Jeff Steinhauer, Carlos Barcelo, Matt Visser._
- **Wave-packet / mode-tracking analyses** (`analytic_classical`) — Track the relevant modes carefully to show the trans-Planckian region is not essential. _Proponents: Renaud Parentani, Ralf Schutzhold._

- **Particle Creation by Black Holes** (1975) — S. W. Hawking. _foundational_, `analytic_classical`. doi:10.1007/BF02345020 — Commun. Math. Phys. 43, 199-220 (1975). The original derivation of black-hole thermal emission; the infinite blueshift of outgoing modes traced backward to the horizon is the very feature that creates the trans-Planckian problem.
- **Experimental Black-Hole Evaporation?** (1981) — W. G. Unruh. _foundational_, `analytic_classical`. doi:10.1103/PhysRevLett.46.1351 — Phys. Rev. Lett. 46, 1351 (1981). Founding paper of analogue gravity: sound waves in a transonic fluid flow form a sonic horizon emitting thermal phonons, opening the route to laboratory tests of the Hawking effect's robustness.
- **Black-hole evaporation and ultrashort distances** (1991) — T. Jacobson. _foundational_, `open_conjecture`. doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.44.1731 — Phys. Rev. D 44, 1731 (1991). One of the first explicit formulations of the trans-Planckian problem, arguing Hawking's derivation relies on arbitrarily short-distance (trans-Planckian) field modes.
- **Sonic analog of black holes and the effects of high frequencies on black hole evaporation** (1995) — W. G. Unruh. _foundational_, `numerical_approximation`. doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.51.2827 — Phys. Rev. D 51, 2827 (1995). Showed via a modified (dispersive) dispersion relation in the sonic-analogue model that the thermal Hawking spectrum is largely insensitive to high-frequency/short-distance physics — the first quantitative robustness result.
- **Hawking radiation without trans-Planckian frequencies** (1995) — R. Brout, S. Massar, R. Parentani, Ph. Spindel. _foundational_, `open_conjecture`. doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.52.4559 — Phys. Rev. Analytic treatment of Hawking emission with a dispersive cutoff showing the thermal flux can be recovered without invoking trans-Planckian modes; a foundational robustness analysis (Brout et al.).
- **Hawking radiation and the trans-Planckian problem in dispersive models / Hawking spectrum and high frequency dispersion** (1996) — S. Corley, T. Jacobson. _foundational_, `numerical_approximation`. doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.54.1568 — Phys. Rev. D 54, 1568 (1996). Canonical lattice/dispersive-field computation confirming the near-thermal Hawking spectrum survives a modified high-frequency dispersion relation; the standard reference for the Corley-Jacobson method.

Tanaka. _foundational_, `numerical_approximation`. https://consensus.app/papers/details/8c39ffa1ba065097ba3b3f0cbcfc8953/ — Phys. Rev. D. ## Key physicists

- **Stephen Hawking** (originator, 1942-2018, University of Cambridge) — Predicted black-hole thermal radiation (1975); his derivation's reliance on infinitely blueshifted modes is the source of the trans-Planckian problem.
- **William G. Unruh** (originator, contemporary, University of British Columbia) — Founded analogue gravity (1981) and showed (1995) the Hawking spectrum is largely robust against high-frequency modifications via the sonic-analogue dispersive model.
- **Ted Jacobson** (foundational, contemporary, University of Maryland) — Sharpened the trans-Planckian problem (1991) and co-developed the Corley-Jacobson lattice/dispersive computation of the modified Hawking spectrum.
- **Renaud Parentani** (foundational, 1962-2020, Universite Paris-Sud (Orsay)) — Leading theorist of dispersive/analogue Hawking radiation; co-author of the Brout-Massar-Parentani-Spindel robustness analyses and S-matrix treatments of short-distance dispersion.
- **Matt Visser** (foundational, contemporary, Victoria University of Wellington) — Co-author of the standard 'Analogue Gravity' Living Reviews; developed the acoustic-metric formalism underpinning analogue Hawking studies.
- **Stefano Liberati** (active, contemporary, SISSA, Trieste) — Co-author of 'Analogue Gravity'; active on Hawking-effect robustness under Lorentz violation, universal horizons, and tunneling methods in dispersive settings.
- **Carlos Barcelo** (active, contemporary, Instituto de Astrofisica de Andalucia (IAA-CSIC)) — Lead author of the 'Analogue Gravity' reviews; works on using avoidance of the trans-Planckian problem as a guide to black-hole evaporation scenarios.
- **Jeff Steinhauer** (active, contemporary, Technion - Israel Institute of Technology) — Performed the headline BEC analogue-black-hole experiments (2014 black-hole laser; 2016 quantum/entangled Hawking radiation) testing the effect's robustness in the lab.
- **Iacopo Carusotto** (active, contemporary, INO-CNR BEC Center, University of Trento) — Pioneered numerical (Gross-Pitaevskii) simulations of analogue Hawking radiation and the density-correlation signature used to detect it.
- **Silke Weinfurtner** (active, contemporary, University of Nottingham) — Led the water-wave (white-hole) experiments demonstrating the thermal/stimulated Hawking conversion, probing the classical robustness of the effect.
- **Ulf Leonhardt** (active, contemporary, Weizmann Institute of Science) — Optical-analogue (fiber/horizon) Hawking-radiation researcher and prominent critic scrutinizing the statistical significance of analogue-Hawking observation claims.
